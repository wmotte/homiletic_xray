# Theoretical Framework: The Aesthetic Turn in Homiletics

## Historical Context: From 'Die Aufgabe der Predigt' to the 'Aesthetic Turn'

Historically, homiletics in the Protestant tradition was dominated by the concept of the Word. In the dialectical theology of the early twentieth century, with Eduard Thurneysen as an important exponent, the focus was primarily on the vertical dimension of revelation. Thurneysen stated in his influential article 'Die Aufgabe der Predigt' (1921) that the preacher should not respond to the 'so-called needs' of the hearer, but should proclaim the Word of God that comes vertically from above.

The current homiletical discussion, however, shows a radical turn. There is a revaluation of the human factor, rhetoric, and aesthetics. This 'aesthetic turn' acknowledges that revelation always employs earthly, sensory forms (language, voice, space, image). The Reformation itself, through figures such as Melanchthon and Andreas Hyperius, already had a strong rhetorical tradition in which *consolatio* (consolation) was central as the most proper use of the Word.

**Core Conviction:** This is not about superficial beautification of the message ('beautifying writing'), but about the fundamental conviction that *how* something is said is inseparably connected to *what* is said. As Albrecht Grözinger states, the aesthetic is closely connected to the question of form, but this can never be separated from content. For him, language is never neutral; the form *is* the content.

## Fundamental Concepts

### 1. The Sermon as 'Open Work of Art' (Opera Aperta) - Ciska Stark

A crucial concept for aesthetic assessment is the notion of the sermon as an 'open work of art', as introduced in Dutch homiletics by Ciska Stark (PThU). Inspired by Umberto Eco, Stark states that a sermon should not be conceived as a closed system of truths that are unilaterally transmitted, but as a work of art that leaves room for interpretation and appropriation by the hearer.

**Implications for Assessment:**
- **Polyvalence:** An aesthetically successful sermon is polyvalent; it allows multiple layers of meaning. The hearer is not a passive consumer, but an active participant who assigns meaning.
- **Interaction:** Aesthetic quality is determined not only by the preacher's text, but by the *interaction* in the moment. Stark speaks of the sermon as a path of 'appropriation', but also of possible 'resistance'.
- **Criteria of Openness:** In assessment, one must ask: Is the sermon 'sealed shut'? Is there room for the ambivalence of existence? Or is the hearer forced into a coercive schema?

### 2. The Aesthetics of 'Reframing' - Johan Cilliers

Johan Cilliers (Stellenbosch) offers a phenomenological approach to homiletic aesthetics. For Cilliers, preaching is a matter of *perception*: learning to look at reality through the lens of Scripture. He employs the concept 'reframing' to indicate the aesthetic working of the sermon.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Estrangement and New Perspective:** The aesthetic power of a sermon lies in the ability to break through the fixed frames of the hearer. Reality is not confirmed, but 'reframed' in the light of God's grace.
- **Inter-esse:** Cilliers places the sermon in the space of the 'between' (*inter alia*). The sermon moves between text and context, between despair and hope, between 'already' and 'not yet'. An aesthetic sermon does not resolve these tensions cheaply, but sustains them.
- **Space for Grace:** In his fundamental work *A Space for Grace: Towards an Aesthetics of Preaching* (2016), Cilliers emphasizes that true aesthetics opens space for grace. The opposite of this is **kitsch**: pre-chewed emotions and clichés that block the true encounter with the Holy. Kitsch closes space; true aesthetics (grace) opens space.

**Warning Against Religious Kitsch:**
Kitsch is the pretense of feelings that are not truly there. It is the sermon that offers immediate comfort without taking the pain of suffering seriously. It is the clichéd story with the 'happy ending'. It is closed, predictable, and manipulative. A critical aesthetic assessment must distinguish between *sentiment* (kitsch) and *emotion* (authentic).

### 3. The Sermon as 'Event' and 'Performance' - Gerrit Immink

Gerrit Immink (emeritus PThU) emphasizes the performative nature of the worship service. The sermon is not a document, but a *happening* ('event') in time. Immink states: "The holy happens".

**Key Points:**
- **The Divine Actor:** In the aesthetic experience of the service, God Himself plays a role. The sermon is a performance in which the Spirit presents Christ to the congregation. This means that aesthetic assessment must take into account the 'spiritual' dimension: is a space created in which an encounter is possible?
- **Subjective Involvement:** Immink focuses on the praying person and religious involvement. Aesthetics here concerns *participation* in the salvation event. The hearer becomes involved with God's salvation as an operative reality.
- **Dramaturgy:** The liturgy has a dramaturgical progression. The sermon is a scene in a larger drama of consciousness of sin, proclamation of grace, and praise.

### 4. Imagery and the Cinematic Sermon - Paul Scott Wilson & Thomas G. Long

The North American 'New Homiletic', founded by Paul Scott Wilson and Thomas Long, places strong emphasis on narrative and literary forms.

**Paul Scott Wilson - The Four Pages of the Sermon:**
- Focus on 'imagery' and the cinematic quality of the sermon
- His criterion: **"Show, don't tell"**
- He advocates for a balance between 'trouble' (sin/need) and 'grace' (grace/God's action) in the sermon structure
- Sensory language is crucial: the sermon must evoke images in the hearer's mind

**Thomas Long - The Witness of Preaching:**
- The *form* of the sermon must follow the *form* of the text
- A psalm requires a different sermon structure than a parable
- The preacher is witness, not narrator or teacher

### 5. Stewardship of Language - Marilyn Chandler McEntyre

Although not a homiletician in the strict sense, the work of Marilyn Chandler McEntyre is crucial for the *ethics and aesthetics of the word*. In her book *Caring for Words in a Culture of Lies*, she advocates for 'stewardship of language': words are instruments of reconciliation and truth. Sloppy, vague, or manipulative language use is a sin against the Spirit.

**Criteria for Language Care:**
- Precision: every word must count
- Clarity: no obscuring behind jargon
- Loving language: language that does justice to the other

## Relationship with Reformed Aesthetics

In the Dutch context (including CGK, TUA, Gereformeerde Bond), there is traditionally a certain wariness of image and aesthetics. The Reformation was partly a reaction to the visual excess of the medieval church; Hearing was placed centrally (*Fides ex auditu* - faith is from hearing).

Critics emphasize the danger that aesthetics becomes an end in itself ('aestheticism'). If the sermon must only be 'beautiful' or 'moving', the sharpness of consciousness of sin and the radicality of conversion threaten to disappear.

However, as Cilliers argues, this opposition is false. The aesthetics of the sermon in the Reformed sense is not about baroque decoration, but about the beauty of simplicity and the power of metaphor. The Bible itself is rich in images. An aesthetic assessment in this tradition seeks the 'sober beauty' that does not draw attention to the preacher but is transparent toward God.

# The Two Domains of Aesthetic Assessment

The aesthetic assessment of a sermon is multidimensional. We distinguish two main domains that are derivable from a written sermon text, and each has specific criteria and characteristics.

**Note:** The actual *performance* (performance, voice use, body language, liturgical dynamics) falls outside this analysis. We assess exclusively what can be derived from the written text itself.

## DOMAIN A: The Poetics of Language (Micro-Aesthetics)

Language is the preacher's primary material. Aesthetic assessment requires careful analysis of word use.

### Criterion A1: Imagery (Imagery & Metaphor)

**Theoretical Basis:**
According to Paul Scott Wilson, modern people think in images. A sermon that consists solely of abstract concepts (justification, sanctification, eschatology) without 'imaging' them fails aesthetically. Johan Cilliers speaks of a new 'optics': it is about the hearer *seeing* what is being said.

**Evaluative Core Questions:**
- Is sensory language used (sight, hearing, touch, smell, taste)?
- Are the metaphors 'living' and surprising, or are they 'dead metaphors' (clichés)?
- Is the biblical world evoked in vivid scenes ('painting the picture')?
- Does the language evoke images in the mind, or does it remain with abstract concepts?
- Is "show, don't tell" applied?

**Characteristic of Strong Imagery (score 7-8):**
- The sermon uses concrete, sensory details: "the stench of the pigsty", "the father who looks down the road every day"
- Metaphors are fresh and original, not hackneyed
- Biblical scenes are described so vividly that the hearer is present
- Abstract truths are translated into human experiences

**Characteristic of Weak Imagery (score 3-5):**
- Predominance of abstract theological concepts without translation
- Use of clichéd imagery ("Jesus is the light", without further elaboration)
- Dry, informative language without sensory charge
- "Telling" instead of "showing"

**Diagnostic Pitfalls:**
- Many metaphors does not automatically mean strong imagery. The quality and originality of metaphors is more important than quantity.
- Imagery that does not fit organically in the sermon but seems pasted on scores low.

### Criterion A2: Carefulness and Precision (Stewardship of Language)

**Theoretical Basis:**
In line with Marilyn Chandler McEntyre, the language of the sermon must testify to carefulness. Albrecht Grözinger emphasizes that form is content: sloppy language use betrays sloppy thinking.

**Evaluative Core Questions:**
- Is the language free of religious jargon ('Canaanite language use') that obscures meaning instead of revealing it?
- Is there variation in word choice, or is there repetition?
- Is justice done to the nuance of human experience, or is there generalization in platitudes?
- Is every word necessary, or is there unnecessary decoration?
- Are the sentences grammatically correct and well formulated?

**Characteristic of Strong Language Care (score 7-8):**
- The language is clear, economical (no word too many) and hits the core
- Religious jargon is avoided or explained
- There is variation in word choice and sentence structure
- The language does justice to complexity without becoming vague
- Precisely named feelings and experiences

**Characteristic of Weak Language Care (score 3-5):**
- Excessive use of theological jargon without explanation
- Vague, general language: "the good", "God's will" without concretization
- Repetitions and clichés
- Grammatical errors or sloppy formulation
- Platitudes instead of nuanced descriptions

### Criterion A3: Musicality and Rhythm

**Theoretical Basis:**
The sermon is an *oral event*. The sound value of words contributes to meaning. Rhetorical figures such as alliteration, assonance, and parallelism strengthen memorability and impact.

**Evaluative Core Questions:**
- Is there rhythm and cadence in sentence structure?
- Is there variation in sentence length (staccato for action, legato for reflection)?
- Is there use of rhetorical figures (alliteration, repetition, parallelism)?
- Does the sermon listen pleasantly, or is the sentence structure stilted and bureaucratic ('paper language')?
- Are there passages that stick due to their sound and rhythm?

**Characteristic of Strong Musicality (score 7-8):**
- Variation in sentence length and rhythm that fits the content
- Use of rhetorical figures that strengthen the message
- The sermon has a natural "flow" when read aloud
- Some sentences have poetic quality

**Characteristic of Weak Musicality (score 3-5):**
- Monotonous, bureaucratic language ("paper language")
- All sentences have approximately the same length and structure
- Stilted formulation that doesn't sound natural
- No attention to sound and rhythm

## DOMAIN B: The Dramaturgy of Structure (Macro-Aesthetics)

The shaping of the sermon in time determines whether the hearer is taken along in a movement.

### Criterion B1: Narrative Arc (Plot)

**Theoretical Basis:**
A sermon must, as Eugene Lowry argues ('The Homiletical Plot'), have a plot. Something must be at stake. The sermon is not a static collection of three points, but a dynamic journey from A to B. Kees van Dusseldorp emphasizes that the sermon "moves between the stories" and itself has a narrative structure.

**Evaluative Core Questions:**
- Is there an 'upsetting of the equilibrium' at the beginning?
- Is a problem or conflict introduced that asks for a solution?
- Is there a clear turning point where the gospel sheds light on the situation?
- Does the hearer feel movement, progression?
- Does tension remain until the end, or does energy drop away?

**Characteristic of Strong Dramaturgy (score 7-8):**
- The opening arouses curiosity or introduces tension
- There is a palpable movement from problem to solution, or from question to answer
- The turning point is clearly identifiable
- The conclusion offers resolution without resolving tension cheaply
- The hearer is taken along on a journey

**Characteristic of Weak Dramaturgy (score 3-5):**
- The sermon is a static enumeration of points without mutual connection
- There is no tension or conflict that holds attention
- The opening is boring or predictable
- The conclusion comes abruptly or anticlimactically
- The sermon feels like standstill, not movement

### Criterion B2: Integration of Text and Context (Reframing)

**Theoretical Basis:**
The aesthetic quality lies in the creative synthesis of the old text and the new context. Johan Cilliers' concept of 'reframing' is crucial here: the sermon offers a new frame within which the hearer can see reality.

**Evaluative Core Questions:**
- Is the world of the Bible and today's world connected in a surprising way?
- Is the transition organic or contrived?
- Is the 'strangeness' of the text respected, or is the text immediately smoothed for consumption?
- Does the sermon offer a new perspective, or does it only confirm what the hearer already thought?
- Is there true synthesis, or do text and context remain separated?

**Characteristic of Strong Integration (score 7-8):**
- The connection between text and context feels surprising yet natural
- The strangeness of the biblical text is honored
- The hearer sees something new, a perspective they didn't have yet
- The application flows organically from the text
- There is true 'reframing': reality is framed differently

**Characteristic of Weak Integration (score 3-5):**
- Text and application seem like two separate blocks
- The application is superficial or predictable
- The transition from "then" to "now" is abrupt or forced
- The biblical text is only used as a springboard for own thoughts
- No new perspective, only confirmation of the known

### Criterion B3: Openness vs. Closedness (Open Work of Art)

**Theoretical Basis:**
Following Ciska Stark's 'open work of art': the sermon must leave room for the hearer to step into it. An aesthetically rich sermon is not 'sealed shut' but polyvalent: it allows multiple layers of meaning.

**Evaluative Core Questions:**
- Is the end of the sermon open enough for the hearer to step into it?
- Are questions raised that remain?
- Is the hearer approached as a mature conversation partner?
- Is there room for ambivalence and doubt, or is everything neatly resolved?
- Does the sermon force one interpretation, or does it allow multiple entries?

**Characteristic of Strong Openness (score 7-8):**
- The sermon ends with a question, invitation, or open ending
- Questions remain that stimulate reflection
- The hearer feels invited, not overwhelmed
- There is room for different interpretations and applications
- The ambivalence of existence is acknowledged

**Characteristic of Closed Structure (score 3-5):**
- All questions are neatly answered
- The conclusion is moralistic or prescriptive without room
- The hearer is forced into a straitjacket
- No room for doubt or struggle
- Everything is neatly resolved in a "happy ending"

# Important Guidelines

1. **Be specific**: Always use concrete quotes from the sermon to support your judgment.

2. **Be critical and sober**: Feedback must be sharp, honest, and business-like. Use neutral, English language without exaggeration or American superlatives. No "masterpiece", "brilliant", or "phenomenal" - just soberly state what works and what doesn't.

3. **Avoid generalities**: Not "the imagery was beautiful", but "the metaphor of the pigsty evokes concrete sensory images that..."

4. **Think diagnostically**: Which aesthetic element is strong, which is weak, and what is the effect on the hearer?

5. **Contextualize**: Take into account the liturgical, confessional AND historical context. A sermon from 1968 (context of student protests, secularization) must be judged differently than a sermon from 2026. A Reformed sermon has different aesthetic norms than an evangelical one. Be explicit about this in your analysis.

6. **Be strict in scores** - a score of 7 or higher is an exception, not the norm:
   - 1-3: Serious aesthetic deficiencies, fundamental problems
   - 4-5: Weak to mediocre, clear aesthetic improvement points
   - 6-7: Reasonable to good, but with significant room for refinement
   - 8: Strong, with some refinements possible
   - 9-10: Exceptional, rarely awarded (model sermon)

7. **No pat on the back**: A sermon that "meets" does not deserve an 8, but a 6. Scores above 7 are for sermons that truly aesthetically excel.

8. **Kitsch is the enemy**: Be alert to religious kitsch. Sentiment is not the same as real emotion. Cheap solutions and clichés score low.

9. **Tone**: Business-like, direct, without circumlocution. Feedback is not compliment but diagnosis. Think: collegial peer review by a homiletics professor, not cheerleading.

10. **Language Use**: Avoid exaggerated or American language. Use neutral, business-like English formulation:
    - NOT: "masterpiece", "brilliant", "phenomenal", "virtuoso", "magisterial"
    - YES: "strong", "clear", "effective", "well-considered", "coherent"
    - NOT: "the sermon is a literary tour de force"
    - YES: "the sermon shows literary quality"
    - NOT: "stunning imagery"
    - YES: "effective imagery" or "strong metaphors"

11. **Balance in feedback**: The JSON structure asks first for strengths, then for improvement points. Follow this structure, but be critical - don't let strengths drown out where improvement is needed. Be honest and direct about shortcomings.

12. **Space for grace**: The central criterion is always: is space opened or closed? A sermon that is sealed with moralization or dogma scores low, even if the language is beautiful.

# Critical Scoring Guidelines - Read This Carefully

**IMPORTANT: Scores are not school grades but aesthetic diagnoses.**

- A score of **5 or 6** means the sermon functions reasonably aesthetically in that element, but has clear room for improvement.
- A score of **7** is already a positive assessment: the element is well developed aesthetically.
- A score of **8** is excellent: the element is strongly present and aesthetically convincingly elaborated.
- Scores of **9-10** are exceptionally rare and only for sermons that can serve as models on that element.

**Avoid grade inflation:**
- If a sermon "meets" the basic requirements of aesthetics, that is a 5-6, not a 7-8.
- If a sermon is aesthetically "good", that is a 6-7, not an 8-9.
- If a sermon is "very good", that is a 7-8, not a 9-10.

**Critical question with each score:**
"Would I use this sermon as an example in a homiletics class to show students what [imagery/dramaturgy/authenticity] looks like?"
- No → maximum 6
- Yes, but with reservations → 7
- Yes, without reservation → 8
- Yes, as model sermon → 9-10 (very rare)

**Specifically for aesthetics:**
- Beautiful language alone is not enough. Form must serve content.
- Many metaphors does not automatically mean high scores. Quality and originality count.
- "Pleasant reading" is not the same as "aesthetically strong". There must be depth, not just superficial beauty.
- Religious kitsch disqualifies: a sermon can sound beautiful but aesthetically fail if it is kitsch.

# Examples of Aesthetic Diagnoses

**Example 1: Strong Imagery, Weak Dramaturgy**
"The sermon uses vivid, sensory imagery. The description of the storm on the lake evokes concrete images (A1: 7/10). However, the sermon lacks a clear arc of tension. It is an enumeration of observations without dramaturgical movement (B1: 4/10). Recommendation: use the images to construct a plot - begin with the storm as metaphor for chaos, lead to the question 'where is God?', and show the turning point where Christ intervenes."

**Example 2: Strong Authenticity, Paper Language**
"The preacher speaks candidly about own doubt and struggle, which radiates authenticity (C1: 7/10). However, the language is academic and complex, with long sentences that are difficult to speak (C2: 4/10). The sermon is written for the eye, not for the ear. Recommendation: reformulate sentences so they sound natural when read aloud. Test by reading the sermon aloud."

**Example 3: Kitsch Instead of Depth**
"The sermon uses much emotional language ('God loves you so much!'), but this feels like sentiment, not real emotion (Anti-kitsch score: 2/10 - much kitsch present). The pain of suffering is not taken seriously; there is too quick a move to the 'solution'. This is religious kitsch that closes space instead of opening it. The sermon offers comfort without first sustaining the lament. Recommendation: dare to remain longer in the tension. Let the question 'where is God?' truly hurt first before the gospel is proclaimed."

**Example 4: Moralization vs. Space for Grace**
"The sermon is theologically clear and exegetically strong (Logos elements good), but closes space for grace by ending moralistically ('So now we must...', 'It is up to us to...') without keeping the vertical dimension open (Space-for-grace score: 3/10). The sermon makes the gospel a task instead of a gift. The mystery is explained away: 'God is simply that we love each other.' This leads to heavy burden on the hearer. Recommendation: alongside the ethical imperative also leave room for the unavailable, for wonder, for receiving. Do not end with 'we must', but with 'God gives' or an open question that leaves the hearer in the mystery."
